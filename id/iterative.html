Iterative Design

Overview

The iterative design project is an assignment from Brown’s CS1300 course, focused particularly on iterative design, prototyping, and usability testing. Working in a team of two, we created and iterated on designs for the UI of a start-up, conducting critque sessions and user usability testing to gain feedback and improve upon each iteration.

The Start-Up

The start-up our team chose to focus on is PiePacker, a free online gaming platform to hangout and play premium retro games with friends online. The user is presented with a collection of original multiplayer games, which they can play in ‘rooms’ similarly to a screen sharing in a Zoom Call. In our case, we chose to design a website interface.

Research

Users

The user who we designed the webpage for are individuals who enjoy computer games and playing these games with friends in a more casual environment. It's aimed towards users who are looking to play games and socially interact others. We also assumed that our users are not necessarily "tech-savvy". Because of this, we wanted our user experience to be as intuitive and easy-to-learn as possible, especially for first-time users.

PiePacker’s goal is to create a fun and social environment for individuals to collaborate and compete in online games, which we chose our user as an individual who is a casual gamer, social, and not necessarily super tech-savvy.

Low-Fidelity Sketches

To start off our design process, we were to create four low-fidelity sketches of our webpage. However, before we could do so, we first identified the inital key pages to design: the home page, game search, login/signup, game room, game results, and settings.

From our initial sketches, we identified the components and designs we liked the most, combining them into our mid-fidelity wireframes.

User Feedback

In our studio critique, we had classmates and a UX researcher from Nintendo interact with our prototype, and gained their feedback. From the session, the most helpful and common feedback points were:

  • Assume users have never seen or used this platform before. The key is to create a site that is very intuitive and learnable.

  • Simplify the Navigation bar so that navigating between pages is less confusing:
    Home, Search, Messages, Account Settings

  • The Leave Game overlay is very confusing. People did not know how to get back to the home page from the game room from the leave button because of the Leave Game overlay.

User Tesing

With our second iteration, we conducted usability testing with three random individuals through UserTesting.com. The link to the three user test videos can be found here.

For our usability testing, we had the users complete a series of sub-tasks to create and enter a game room:

1. Create an Account
2. Choose a game from favorite games list
3. Create a room
4. Join a game
5. Look at game Chat
6. Leave Game and Return to Home
7. Look at your chat notifications and open up the most recent conversation
8. Go to Account Settings and log out

User A

Time to complete: ~4:00 min
“Really nothing [was confusing]. Tasks were very easy as if it were a site I was familiar with”

User A was able to complete the tasks with ease, and found the site both straightforward and intuitive. They also said they liked the purple and gray color scheme.

User B

Time to complete: ~9:00 min
“I had a hard time finding home and basically back tracking through anything.”

During User B’s test, they were confused by Figma’s prototype and accidently ended up signing up for Figma. They ran into issues with going back home, although for the rest of the prototype they said it was fairly intuitive.

User C

Time to complete: ~3:30 min
“The layout of the site was very basic and did not look professional.”

User C was able to complete tasks relatively easily. However, when creating a room, they ran into an issue where they were unable to close the overlay, and had the restart. They also recommended making the appearance more professional.


The results showed the usability and intuitiveness of our design backed up by the positive user feedback. Users B and C ran into issues with closing out the overlays, but this was an error on our team’s end; we did not connect the prototype correctly (accidently selected ‘navigate to’ instead of ‘swap with’ for the overlays). We also did not link the back button on the game information page, another design error on our end.

Using the feedback we recieved, we corrected the prototyping errors that users experienced. If given more time, we would definitely continue to refine the appearance of the website, making it more professional and making components more compact.

Takeaways

Throughout this project, our biggest takeaway was the experience we gained conducting usability testing through a professional site like Usertesting.com, as well as how to structure usability tests to best gauge results. Subtasks gave the users enough direction to move through the site, but also left room for them to figure things out on their own and see what issues they experienced.

The Nintendo UX Researcher told us that our start-up interface was one that was relatively new, which gave us much more freedom to explore and try things out. As a result, we had much more space for creativity. However, because iof this, the key question was: how we could make our users understand how to use the site. In other words, when dealing with new and innovative ideas, having intuitive and understandable interfaces is key to maintaining usability and minimizing confusion.
